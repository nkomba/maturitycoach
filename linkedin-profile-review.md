The opening ("The leaders I work with are navigating uncharted territory") is a genuine hook, the paradox framing is sharp, the "What makes my coaching different" and "My clients include" segments do real qualifying work, and the closing CTA is clear. Two small fixes:

### Experience — your weakest section, biggest opportunity
The roles and tenure are solid, but the descriptions hurt you:

- **Generic, "AI-written" bullets.** Phrases like *"resulting in measurable improvements in productivity"* and *"fostering a culture of innovation and excellence"* appear across multiple roles and say nothing concrete. For your current coaching role especially, replace these with **specifics**: types of leaders coached, a named transformation, a result with a number, or a client outcome ("coached a CTO through a 200-person org scale-up," etc.). Prospects believe specifics, not adjectives.
- **No client proof in the coaching role.** The Managing Partner entry is where a buyer looks hardest. Add a concrete engagement story or a short client result. Even one vivid example beats three generic bullets.
- **Older roles have no descriptions** (Grant Thornton, Xerox, Alena Systems). That's fine — but a single line tying them to your current story ("deep technology background that gives me boardroom credibility") would convert your career history into an *asset* for the coaching narrative rather than dead weight.
- **Consistency of identity.** The top card and headline lean fully into coaching, but the experience section still reads like a technology-leadership résumé. Decide what each older role is *doing* for your coaching story and trim accordingly.

For a coach whose whole pitch is *wisdom and thought leadership*, silence is the wrong signal. Your comments show you write thoughtfully — turn some of that into posts.
